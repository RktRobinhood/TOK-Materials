How to think (the scientist’s workflow)

1) Define the claim

Write it as: “If X is true, then we should observe Y.” Make it measurable.

2) Find best evidence

Use peer‑reviewed papers, datasets, and reputable scientific agencies.

3) Try to falsify

Actively search for disconfirming evidence and competing explanations.

Choose a research question

Pick one question. Your deck should make your claim testable, then evaluate evidence for and against it.

Q1 — Falsifiability

What is falsifiability, and why is it critical for scientific theories?

  • Keywords: Popper, demarcation, scientific method

Q2 — Predictions & tests

What key global warming predictions have been made, and how are they tested?

  • Keywords: models, observations, uncertainty

Q3 — Challenges

What evidence could potentially challenge aspects of global warming theory?

  • Keywords: alternative explanations, critiques

Q4 — Responses

How do climate scientists respond to critiques regarding falsifiability?

  • Keywords: methodology, robustness checks

Q5 — History

What historical examples exist where climate-related theories were falsified?

  • Keywords: shifting hypotheses, revisions

Q6 — Bias

What role does confirmation bias play in public perception of global warming?

  • Keywords: media framing, cognition

Minimum sourcing rule

Include at least two peer‑reviewed sources and cite every figure, dataset, and non‑trivial claim. AI tools can help you find sources, but you must read/verify the originals.

Free research tools (recommended)

Use these to discover papers and map what the research community says — then read the originals.

🔎

Smart discovery tools

  • Consensus.app — quick overview of peer‑reviewed findings on a question.
  • ResearchRabbit — visual “map” of related papers and authors.
  • Connected Papers (free tier) — paper graph around a seed article.
  • Semantic Scholar — strong filters + citation trails.
  • OpenAlex — open index of research works and citations.
  • Google Scholar — useful for “cited by” and year filters.
Best practice: start with one strong paper → follow “cited by” → compare opposing evidence → write your falsification test.

Slide deck structure (10–12 slides)

Clean, visual-first slides. One message per slide. Use graphs, diagrams, and short headlines.

Suggested slide order

  1. Title (question + names)
  2. Claim / hypothesis (“If X, then Y”)
  3. Background + key terms
  4. How you searched (tools + keywords)
  5. Best supporting evidence (1–2 slides)
  6. Best challenging evidence (1–2 slides)
  7. Falsification test (what would prove it wrong?)
  8. Alternative explanations / limitations
  9. Conclusion (what survives + what’s uncertain)
  10. References

Design rules

  • No paragraphs. If it won’t fit in ~2 short bullets, it’s too much.
  • Prefer figures (with captions) to long explanations.
  • Make titles full sentences (the “takeaway”).
  • Cite sources on every slide with external info (author/year or org + link).
Common mistake: treating news/opinion as scientific evidence. Use it only to analyse rhetoric or bias.
🧪

Required falsification slide

  • Prediction: If our claim is true, we should observe ____.
  • Falsifier: If we instead observe ____, our claim would be wrong (or need revision).
  • Test: Which measurement/dataset could check this?

Grading rubric

Science quality matters more than persuasion. Show your reasoning and engage with counter‑evidence fairly.

Criteria Weight What strong looks like
Understanding 20% Accurate definitions and correct use of scientific terminology; clear explanation of the claim.
Sources & research quality 20% Peer‑reviewed and authoritative sources; citations are transparent and consistent.
Falsification 25% Specific falsifiable prediction; identifies what would change your mind; engages with disconfirming evidence.
Critical evaluation 20% Discusses uncertainty, assumptions, alternative explanations, and limits of methods/models.
Communication & design 15% Visual-first slides; readable layout; balanced speaking roles; clear narrative.
